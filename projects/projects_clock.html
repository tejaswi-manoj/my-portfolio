    • Voice-Controlled Spray Alarm Clock [In Progress]

    EMBEDDED SYSTEMS •
    Raspberry Pi
    Python
    TinkerCAD

    Challenge

    Build a voice-controlled alarm clock that wakes you up with your favorite song and sprays water whenever you try to snooze.

    Background

    I cannot get myself to wake up in the morning, no matter what I try. Keeping my phone 10 feet away, changing the alarm ringtone to my favorite song, sleeping early - nothing seems to work. This is because I believe that alarms are fundamentally flawed because they have the feature of the snooze button. So I decided to build an alarm clock that would wake me up the way my mom used to back in high school: spraying water on me if I ever try to hit snooze.

    Approach

    I thought about all the features I would like my alarm clock to have:

    • It should wake me up without my phone
    • It should emulate how my mom used to wake me up: gently in the beginning (because I don't want to immediately ruin my morning, of course) with a song I like and if I try to hit snooze, it should spray water on me.
    • Setting the alarm time should be as seamless and easy as using my phone, preferably through my voice.

    After I came up with the project requirements, I mapped them to the actual parts I would need:

    • Raspberry Pi: main brain of the clock, running all programs
    • LED Matrix: to show the time
    • LCD Display: to show date, day, and the next alarm set
    • DFPlayer + Speaker: to play my favorite songs, randomized every morning
    • Water Pump + hose + relay module + power supply for pump: to spray water
    • USB Mic: to set the alarm with voice
    • 3D Printed casing: to hold it all together and make it look like an actual product
    • Jumper wires: to connect everything

    I followed simple tutorials online to set up most of the above components. For setting my alarm with my voice, I researched some speech to text models and narrowed my final choice down to Vosk. It’s a lightweight, speech to text model that runs offline and seemed to be a good choice to run on a Raspberry Pi. I had Claude do most of the heavy-lifting for this. For the 3D casing, I used TinkerCAD and a bunch of free 3D models from Printables.com.
